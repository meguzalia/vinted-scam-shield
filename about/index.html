Why Vinted Scam Shield Exists

Vinted is one of the fastest-growing platforms in Europe. As it grows, so does the number of scams on it — and not everyone browsing Vinted is comfortable spotting the warning signs. This is the story of why this extension exists.

Vinted is growing — fast

Vinted has seen explosive growth in recent years, driven by people looking for value as the cost of living rises across Europe. The platform was recently valued at over $9 billion, and millions of new buyers and sellers join every year.

Growth like that is great for the second-hand economy. But it also means more listings, more sellers, and — inevitably — more people trying to take advantage of buyers who don't know what to look for.

Not everyone is tech-savvy

Spend time in Vinted's buyer communities and a pattern becomes obvious. Experienced users develop instincts over time — checking for tag photos, reverse-searching images, reading reviews carefully. But these instincts take time, and often a bad experience, to build.

Many buyers simply don't have the time, the experience, or the technical know-how to run a Google Lens search on every listing photo, or to recognise the subtle signs of an AI-generated description. They just want to buy a jumper without getting a Shein item at triple the price.

"Here's what Vinted has become — Y2K and vintage have become synonymous with Shein, and with scams. You overconsume, then it gets resold for more."

— Buyer, sharing a side-by-side comparison of "vintage" listings and their original Shein product photos

Existing protection comes after the fact

Vinted does offer buyer protection, and it does cover situations like non-arrival or significantly misrepresented items. But protection that activates after a problem has already happened is a poor substitute for not having the problem in the first place.

Disputes take time. They require effort, evidence, and patience. And by the time a dispute is resolved — however it goes — the buyer has already spent money, waited for a delivery, and dealt with the frustration of being misled.

🛡️
Prevention is simply better than cure

The best outcome isn't a successful dispute three weeks later. It's never having the problem at all — because something flagged the listing before you clicked "buy".

The community already knows this

This isn't a niche concern. Threads asking "what did getting scammed on Vinted teach you?" regularly get hundreds of upvotes and dozens of replies — buyers sharing hard-won lessons about tag photos, reverse image searches, and sellers who go quiet the moment you ask a real question.

"I'm not buying any more clothing without seeing a label pic. I also look a lot more carefully at photos now and if I have any doubts, do more research with Google Lens."

— r/vinted community member

The knowledge exists. It's just scattered across forum threads, learned the hard way, one bad purchase at a time. Vinted Scam Shield exists to bring some of that collective knowledge to the moment it actually matters — while you're looking at a listing, before you buy.
